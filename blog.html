Blog #4

Flower Field Games

We've started thinking about different games we might play with this installation. The main story we're talking about is creating waves of light and sound that a participant creates when she walks through the field. That's a game called "waves".

But here's the thing: once we can identify and track a person's movement through the field, and link the movement to the LEDs and sound, we can do lots of different things. We're calling these "games."

Another game could be "aura". We choose one person in the field. All the flowers go dark except the flowers around this one person. As she moves, a pool of light and sound moves with her, not in a wave, but surrounding her. This pool could be of moving colors, swirling and blending across and within flowers, but always surrounding the one person. We might add a second person's aura, then more, until it's a mix of colors across the garden.

A third game is "pollinator." In this game, flowers glow in warm colors (reds, oranges, yellows) and make needy sounds. When a person approaches and pauses near one flower, the flowers shift to cooler colors (blues, greens, and greys) while making gentle sighs of satisfaction. The sighs could become, ahem, more emphatic as the same person pollinates multiple flowers.

We have lots of ideas about games the flowers will play only with each other, without interaction from people. Of course we will need to play Conway's Game of Life, that seems unavoidable. But the flowers could also show patterns of requests and offerings, signaling to each other with sound and responding with light.

So much excitement about this build! It's keeping us going as we explore software libraries, LED types, ESP32 variants, I2s amplifiers, batteries, and speakers.

Blog #5

Tree of Ténéré

2017 was a great Burning Man year for me, for all kinds of reasons, but one of those reasons was the magnificent Tree of Ténéré This massive LED display included around 100,000 LEDs embedded in something like 15,000 leaves. I had amazing conversations under the tree, marveling at the scale and at the patterns.

After the artists took it down they reinstalled it in Oslo

Now called "Oslo Tree," it looks amazing by the fjord.

This means that the patterns have an irregular depth which creates a much richer, more complex and interesting pattern for the eye (relative to a flat plane).

The leaves themselves are an inspiration for our flowers.

The tree's leaves are much thinner (and vastly more numerous) than the leaves we will build for FoF. Our leaves will have a bit more diffusion, but our ideas are similar.

We're getting closer to making final production decisions for FoF. This was an opportune moment to see the Tree again, and to remember how impressive it is: routing information to more than 100,000 LEDs is a terrific feat. And the physical construction of the leaves, branches, and truck set a very high bar for our flowers.

Blog #7

The LEDs are looking great!

We now have nearly-production-ready code for the LEDs. Check out this gorgeous dimming!

This is just brightening and dimming. Of course the flowers will do lots of other things, with complex color patterns in each flower. However, the most fundamental aspect of our project is brightening and dimming as a wave passes over, and it's essential that the lighting is smooth.

We're there. Of course we can (and probably will) continue to tune this for even smoother changes in brightness. However, this is good enough for now. This smoothness means that we can use the cheaper and more available WS2812B LEDs instead of the faster but more expensive APA102 variants (like the new HD107s).

Blog #9

Flower communication

We can now send commands over wifi from a computer to a flower to make it do things. The network communication happens while the LED continues to smoothly brighten and dim, without the LEDs glitching at all.

The real magic here happens because we're using both of the cores in the ESP32 chip (like a CPU but not exactly). The LEDs are updating at around 300 frames per second, and at the same time, the flower is talking to the laptop without delays in either process. If you want to see how we're doing the code, check out our repository here

Next up: make the ESP32 play audio while still running the LEDs and handling the networking. Very exciting progress.

We're working with Makerfabs for our ESP32 and audio amplifier electronics. They have a really nice and inexpensive ESP32 board that even has a little display (we're using the WROVER-B version that has 8MB PSRAM and 16MB flash memory). We're also using their Audio expansion board that connects to this ESP32, but we're modifying it for our needs. Makerfabs has a PCB design service, and they're helping us adapt their board.

The diagram below shows what our modified board will look like. We're adding a level shifter chip (we're trying a 74LVC1T45W6-7), a USB-C input to route 5v power from the battery to the whole flower, and jacks for speaker output and power+data to the LEDs.

Blog #11

Warped Perspectives

One of the first challenges in the computer vision side of this project is to take an image shot from an angle and convert it into a flat plane. This is critical to determine the locations of the flowers and the locations of people moving around them.

Fortunately, some excellent software tools exist to do just that

To figure out how to use them we built a scale replica of the field which we've been referring to as Lego Playa, where each 1 cm square on the paper = 1 ft in the real world (metric, imperial, why not both!). The darker squares on the corners are used for triangulation and the square + dot in the middle is our center-point target.

In the left picture, shot at an angle, our Lego Playa looks like a lop-sided diamond. On the right the image has been run through our program to warp the perspective into a super-convenient, uniform square. The red dot in the center(ish) of the target at 50 x 50 means that our program's idea of center matches the real world's idea of center.

As a bonus, we can use the same perspective transformation to plot the locations of people on the field once we've got a way to spot them in the image. More on that soon.

Blog #12

Computer Vision

The second challenge in the computer vision side of the project is spotting people as they walk through the field. As with the perspective warping there are some awesome tools out there that are built to do exactly that.

In the image above, we successfully spot the lego character on our Lego Playa, and detect the point on the playa (red dot) where they're standing.

Next up will be to do it on video and track their paths through the flowers.

Blog #13

Synchrony

How do we get all the flowers to create light and sound at the same time? This problem is called synchrony. We manage it by having all the little computers in the flowers (called ESP32s) to share a very precise clock. Once they're all on the beat, we can tell them to go at the same time.

Here's a video showing that even without really fancy software, we can create LED blinks within about 50 milliseconds of one another. That's good, but of course we'll keep making it tighter.

Audio is a lot more complicated. We need all the little songs to play on the beat, so we need the flowers to keep a beat that we can use to initiate audio playback in a consistent way. Audio does not play back immediately when we send the command to the ESP32s because we have to read the song file from the flash memory, so there's a little bit of latency. We want to use the latency to get all the ESP32s to start on the same downbeat. A lot more more on this coming soon.

Blog #16

Testing our new PCB

A few weeks ago I wrote about our custom printed circuit boards (PCB) designed and manufactured by Makerfabs. The first samples arrived, and I've started testing them.

The custom boards are a modification of Makerfabs's audio expansion board. The modified PBC includes a jack for power into USB-c that will power all the LEDs; a level shifter chip.; and an amplifier (the MAX98357A). It also includes an output jack for data and power to the LEDs.

First I put the board on the multimeter and the osilloscope (see photo). The multimeter confirms that the board is passing power directly from the battery through USB-c to the +5v/GND leads to power the LEDs. This is good, the same voltage from the battery is coming across the cable to the LEDs, no voltage drop, so there's a clean power plane in the PCB.

Then I put the data lead thru the oscilloscope to check that the level shifter is doing its job: if the level shifter weren't working, we'd see the peaks of the data high signal at 3.3v, which is the voltage that comes out of the ESP32. However, we correctly see a nice steady 5v for high (ok there's a little bounce, don't worry about that, the ESP32 isn't all that crisp at 1MHz). Check: we don't need to worry about the WS2812b chips in the LEDs losing track of the signal.

Note that the oscope shows that data is flowing at about 1MHz. That's just about right for the WS2812b's max 800kbs. So I think that's good too. Great stuff: next up is a test of the wifi and how the boards coordinate with the server software we're developing.

Blog #17

Testing flower communication

The flowers talk to each other and to a server over wifi. We need to start testing how well the wifi works, and to look for bugs in our communications software. So we assembled a few test flowers with the new custom PCBs and Makerfabs's ESP-32, along with a speaker and LEDs in prototype flowers (and LED loops to simulate the leaves).

Next I moved the flowers out 50', 75', and 100' from the router.

The point is to measure the wifi signal strength and make sure we can tell the flowers to do stuff from the server. I've got my shop server running NTP and mosquitto. (which was slightly more trouble than I expected bc security I didn't know my vender included kept blocking them). Here's what the flower-control-center looks like (omg I ❤❤❤ the flower control center!):

The signal strength drops with distance, of course: 50' is totally fine (-45dbm), 75' is weak (-64dbm), and 100' is marginal (-75dbm). I moved the flowers around some, and this result is consistent. While I say "marginal," I was able to send a "playAudio" command to the farthest flower with no trouble (the cheapie bluetooth speakers are *plenty* loud, we're going to have to figure out how to configure them to standard volume on the speaker side). However, I note that the signal that far out is only -75dbm, and we could have dropouts for the flowers far side of the garden on the far side of the router.

If we're worried about signal strength (maybe?), I wonder if we want to put a "beehive" or something in the middle of the field for the router? That would mean a max of ~50' from router to all the flowers, thereby keeping all the flowers at good signal strength? The downside would be that we'd probably want to trench from the flagpole/bird/camera to the router to run an ethernet cable so we get zero dropouts between RPi controller+camera and the router. Anyway, that's a design problem we will keep thinking about.

Blog #18

Curvy stalks

In a previous post ("Stems and Caps"), I wrote about how we're creating the stalks of the flowers by bending the PVC. Here I want to provide a little more detail.

We heat sand in an oven at 550F. Then we pour the hot sand into PVC pipes. When the pipes get hot, they get all floppy and we can push them into forms.

Then we pour out the hot sand and let the pipes cool for about 30 minutes.

It makes a mess, but the results are terrific! We have 170 stalks with pretty bends ready for sanding, painting, drilling, and then assembly with flowers, leaves, and the birdhouse (a blogpost on on birdhouses coming soon).

Blog #19

Production begins

We have 170 stalks made, ready for painting.

And we have 160 metal feet that will support the stalks and be fastened to the playa with lag bolts. The feet were produced by my shop neighbor Miguel Reyes. Miguel is a welder who previously worked on the Flower Tower.

Painting is a little tricky. The primer clogged the sprayer, so (after much discussion with the sprayer manufacturer's tech support) we gave up and primed by hand. The final coats are still in progress.

Meanwhile we cut the bases which are pine 1x8 ripped to 3.5", cut to 8.25", with a hole drilled for the stalk to pass through.

The bases hold a little box that will protect the PCBs, the batteries, and the speakers; we call this box the birdhouse. The sides of the birdhouse are the most complicated part. We cut 4'x8' sheets of plywood into 8.25" strips, then cut four 1/4" dadoes into them, then chopped them to 9", then routed a rabbet across the top so that the tops will seat smoothly into them.

This step had a lot of mistakes. The plywood doesn't really like to have so many grooves cut into it, so about 40% of the panels splintered and must be discarded; I remade them all yesterday. Cutting so much into the plywood maybe wasn't my best design decision.

Now we have to cut holes into all the panels so the speaker's sound will come out. I'm calling this the festival of template routing.

Finally, we started cutting 1/8" Lauan strips for the internal dividers. These 4x8' sheets are too floppy and thin to cut on the table saw, so we set up a straight edge and cut the strips with a circular saw (with a finely-toothed blade). They turned out better than I expected.

So it's all going! We have tons more little tasks getting done, and I can really feel the momentum now. Very exciting!

Blog #19

Bombus Nevadensis

A golden deluge of morning sunlight poured into the valley, casting an amber glow across many rocky outcroppings while throwing myriad recesses into deep, umber shadows. The final exertions of winter’s frigid gifts rested lightly along the slopes, yet the landscape had already begun to experience the exuberant quickening of spring. Near a weather-worn rock face, emerging from a snug burrow after having patiently overwintered therein, a tiny creature gingerly ventured forth. Vibrant yellow collided beautifully with stripes of jet, glossy and robust, existing in stark contrast to the muted environment surrounding its burrow. Antennae lazily danced while six legs, long ago addled by freezing temperatures, regained their strength. Gossamer wings began the long process of engorging, fueled by the glycol-rich blood coursing through her body. She is able to rouse herself much earlier than her fellow pollinators due to her wonderfully adapted physiology and, as such, has her pick of the most delectable blossoms of early spring here in Nevada…

The Nevada bumblebee is a species of flying insect that ranges across much of western North America. Like many bumblebees across the globe, she enjoys early spring feeding due to the presence of increased levels of glycol in her blood. This chemical allows bumblebees to overwinter without freezing. Bumblebees are covered in hairy structures called ‘setae’ that give them their iconic furry appearance and lend to the erroneous belief that bumblebees should not be able to fly. Bumblebees are responsible for the pollination of many native plant species, as well as major crop resources including but not limited to tomatoes, potatoes, and eggplant. Bumblebees are becoming ever more popular both within and without professional greenhouse operations due to their special relationship with these important food sources.
